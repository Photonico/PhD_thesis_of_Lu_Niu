\chapter[Optoelectronic Properties of Heterostructure Bilayers]{Electronic and Optical Properties of 2D Heterostructure Bilayers of Graphene, Borophene and 2D Boron Carbides from First-Principles}
\label{cha:project_1}

\section{Abstract}

In this chapter,
we investigate the atomic, electronic, and optical properties of two-dimensional 
namely Graphene-\ce{BC3}, Graphene-Borophene, and Graphene-\ce{B4C3},
as well as their constituent monolayers, on the basis of first-principles calculations using the HSE06 hybrid functional.
The calculations show that while borophene is metallic,
both monolayer \ce{BC3} and \ce{B4C3} are indirect semiconductors,
with band gaps of \qty{1.822}{\electronvolt} and \qty{2.381}{\electronvolt} as obtained using HSE06.
The Graphene-\ce{BC3} and Graphene-\ce{B4C3} bilayer heterojunction systems maintain the Dirac point of graphene at the $\mathrm{K}$ point and are semimetals,
while Graphene-Borophene is metallic.
All bilayer heterostructure systems exhibit absorption in the visible region, where the resonance frequency and resonance absorption peak intensity vary between structures.
In addition, all heterojunctions support plasmons within the range \qtyrange{16.5}{18.5}{\electronvolt}, while Graphene-\ce{B4C3} and Graphene-Borophene exhibit a $\pi$-type plasmon within the region \qtyrange{4}{6}{\electronvolt},
with the latter possessing an additional plasmon at the lower energy of \qtyrange{1.5}{3}{\electronvolt}.
The dielectric tensor for Graphene-\ce{B4C3} exhibits complex off-diagonal elements due to the lower $\mathrm{P}3$ space-group symmetry, indicating anisotropic dielectric properties and possible optically active (chiral) effects.
These results show that the two-dimensional heterostructures have desirable optical properties and broaden their potential applications.

\section{Introduction}

Graphene has excellent electrical conductivity,
desirable mechanical and thermal properties,
and high light transmittance in the visible-light and infrared regions.
It also has an extremely high quantum efficiency for light-matter interactions and is strongly optically nonlinear.
These properties have therefore enabled applications in e.g. electronics, energy storage, biomedical devices, and other fields~\cite{janavika2023graphene}.
Relative to conventional plasmonic materials, graphene possesses highly confined plasmons with much longer lifetimes.
In addition, graphene plasmons are active in an extended wavelength range, namely, the mid-infrared and terahertz regime.
This wavelength range overlaps with the characteristic signals of most organic and biomolecules,
which has broadened graphene's applications towards plasmonic biological and chemical sensors~\cite{sakthivel2023} in addition to plasmonic waveguides
and infrared photodetectors.
Moreover, forming graphene-hybrid materials can tune the properties of graphene, finding applications in biosensors, chemical sensors, optical sensors, and other types of sensors~\cite{zhang2022}.

In other areas, however, the use of graphene remains limited by its zero band gap.
One method used to expand the application of graphene is to form heterostructures.
Stacking different two-dimensional (2D) materials together can form double-layer or even multi-layer artificial materials that are maintained by van der Waals interactions~\cite{novoselov20162d}.
Such materials are known as van der Waals heterojunctions.
Such stacking can generate a wide range of physical properties,
making van der Waals heterojunctions even more important than the individual 2D materials themselves.
There are vast numbers of 2D materials, and by forming heterostructures thereof,
a diverse range of electrical, photonic, plasmonic, mechanical, and chemical properties can be theoretically predicted through \textit{ab initio} calculations and machine learning~\cite{thygesen2017,he2023}, and experimentally engineered~\cite{lin2023recent,elbanna20232d}.

Recently, the prospect of developing plasmonic devices employing 2D semiconductors has attracted considerable attention~\cite{agarwal2018plasmonics, qiu2018optical}.
Plasmonic modes in each class of van der Waals semiconductors have their own peculiarities, along with potential technological capabilities.
Graphene-like \ce{BC3} has also been synthesized experimentally~\cite{yanagisawa2004phonon, yanagisawa2006phonon, tanaka2005novel} and is found to be semiconducting with an indirect band gap.
Calculations have shown that nanostructures of graphene-like \ce{BC3} exhibit desirable absorption in the visible region and, by changing the size of the nanostructure,
the resonance peak position of the absorption spectrum can be effectively regulated~\cite{chen2019plasmon}.
Recent studies have also predicted a stable \ce{B4C3} monolayer,
which is even more stable than the previously synthesized \ce{BC3} monolayer~\cite{tian2019}.
Boron carbides are known for their exceptional hardness,
thermal stability, and chemical inertness.
The emergence of 2D boron carbides adds a new dimension to the study of these materials,
offering novel properties and opportunities for technological innovation~\cite{luo2011predicting,fan2018two}.

Borophene is another 2D material of high current interest.
This monolayer allotrope of boron forms several structures and is characterized by its unique atomic structure and exceptional electronic properties,
which have opened new avenues for innovation in the areas of electronics,
energy storage, catalysis, plasmonics, superconductivity, and sensors~\cite{qiu2018optical}.
One particularly intriguing aspect of borophene's versatility lies in its ability to form heterojunctions---interfaces between different borophene structures or between borophene and other materials.
Borophene-graphene heterostructures have been realized~\cite{hou2021borophene},
where they have shown good stability and the ability to function as humidity sensors,
exhibiting a sensitivity about 700 times higher than that of pristine graphene,
and 27 times higher than that of borophene.
Recently, both lateral and vertical integration of borophene with graphene has also been achieved~\cite{liu2019borophene},
as has the formation of bilayer ($\alpha$-phase) borophene on the Ag(111) surface~\cite{liu2022}.

The synergistic combination of graphene's exceptional electrical conductivity,
mechanical strength, and flexibility with boron carbide's high hardness,
thermal stability, and chemical resistance, and with borophene's attractive electronic properties,
holds significant potential for a wide range of applications spanning from electronics and optoelectronics to energy storage and sensing.
As research in this field continues to advance,
the development of scalable synthesis methods, the elucidation of structure-property relationships,
and the exploration of novel applications will be key focal points.
To date, little is known about the physical properties of graphene-based bilayer heterojunctions with borophene and boron-carbide materials.
This motivates the present chapter to gain an understanding of these heterojunctions
through the study of their electronic and optical properties on the basis of first-principles calculations.
Such understanding is crucial for their future technological application,
where the unique properties of these materials can be harnessed.

This chapter is organised as follows:
In Section~\ref{1_calculation_method}, the calculation method is described,
followed by the Results and Discussion in Section~\ref{1_results}.
Herein, we describe the physical properties of the monolayers and heterostructures, including band structure, density of states, electron density difference distributions,
Schottky barriers and the optical properties.
This is followed by the Conclusion in Section~\ref{1_conclusions}.

\section{Calculation methods}
\label{1_calculation_methods}

We perform first-principles calculations using density functional theory (DFT) as implemented in the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)~\cite{kresse1993ab, kresse1996efficiency, kresse1996efficient}.
Projector augmented wave (PAW) pseudopotentials account for the electron-ion interactions~\cite{kresse1999ultrasoft}.
For the structural optimization calculations, we use the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) of Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) as the exchange-correlation functional.
The Becke-Johnson damped D3(BJ) dispersion correction accounts for long-range van der Waals (vdW) interactions.
The structural calculations use a $\Gamma$-centered $\vec{k}$-point sampling mesh of $11 \times 11 \times 1$, except for the smaller graphene unit cell shown in figure~\ref{fig1.1}, for which a denser mesh of $23 \times 23 \times 1$ is used.
Here, the plane-wave energy cutoff is set to \qty{450}{\electronvolt}.
The electronic convergence criterion is set to \qty{1e-6}{\electronvolt}, and the systems are allowed to relax until all forces on each atom are below \qty{0.01}{\electronvolt\per\angstromunit}.
For smearing at the Fermi level,
we use the Gaussian smearing method implemented in VASP.

For the calculation of electronic properties,
the PBE exchange-correlation functional is known to underestimate band gap energies.
Therefore, we also use the screened hybrid exchange-correlation functional of Heyd, Scuseria, and Ernzerhof (HSE06),
which gives more accurate band gap energies~\cite{sassnick2021electronic, heyd2003hybrid, krukau2006influence}.

All density of states (DoS) calculations use a $\Gamma$-centered $\vec{k}$-point sampling mesh of $33 \times 33 \times 1$,
with smearing at the Fermi level calculated using the Gaussian method and a broadening value of \qty{0.1}{\electronvolt}.
The HSE06 exchange-correlation functional is also used for the calculation of optical properties,
in particular, the frequency-dependent dielectric function with $\Gamma$-centered $\vec{k}$-point sampling meshes of $17 \times 17 \times 1$ and $65 \times 65 \times 1$.
The complex frequency-dependent dielectric function, $\varepsilon(\omega)$,
is given by equation~\ref{complex_dielectric_function_definition};
here, $\varepsilon_{1}(\omega)$ and $\varepsilon_{2}(\omega)$ are the real and imaginary parts, respectively, and $\omega$ denotes the photon energy in \unit{\electronvolt}.

We then use the dielectric function to calculate the absorption coefficient,
refractive index, extinction coefficient, reflectivity, and energy-loss spectrum,
following the relations introduced in section~\ref{sec:from_dielectric_function_to_optical_properties}.

The interlayer binding energy $E_{\mathrm{b}}$ follows as:

\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{b}}
= E_{\mathrm{H}} - E_{\mathrm{BC}/\mathrm{B}} - E_{\mathrm{G}}
\label{interlayer_binding_energy}
\end{equation}

here, $E_{\mathrm{H}}$ denotes the total energy of the heterostructure,
$E_{\mathrm{BC}/\mathrm{B}}$ corresponds to the total energy of the isolated boron-carbide or borophene monolayer,
and $E_{\mathrm{G}}$ denotes the total energy of the isolated graphene monolayer.

Afterwards, the charge density difference $\Delta \rho(\vec{r},)$ takes the form:

\begin{equation}
\Delta \rho(\vec{r},) =
\rho_{\mathrm{AB}}(\vec{r},)
- \rho_{\mathrm{A}}(\vec{r},)
- \rho_{\mathrm{B}}(\vec{r},)
\label{charge_density_difference}
\end{equation}

here, $\rho_{\mathrm{AB}}(\vec{r},)$ denotes the total charge density of the heterostructure.
The terms $\rho_{\mathrm{A}}(\vec{r},)$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{B}}(\vec{r},)$ represent the charge densities of the isolated monolayers forming the heterostructure.

\subsection{Heterostructure}

\subsubsection{Structural properties}

We next determine the structures of the bilayer heterojunctions,
which consist of monolayer graphene stacked with monolayer \ce{BC3}, borophene,
and \ce{B4C3}, namely Graphene-\ce{BC3}, Graphene-Borophene, and Graphene-\ce{B4C3}, respectively.
To create the heterostructures, we use a $2\times 2\times 1$ cell.
For each system,
we consider several lateral stacking coordinations of these layers.
Specifically, we consider `Hollow'', `Bridge'', and `Top'' coordinations for the Graphene-\ce{BC3} bilayer system,
and `Hollow 1'', `Hollow 2'', `Bridge'', and ``Top'' coordinations for the Graphene-Borophene and Graphene-\ce{B4C3} bilayer systems.
We calculate the relative energies,
optimized lattice constants, and interlayer distances for all structures.
Figures~\ref{S1.4}--\ref{S1.6} give details of this procedure.
Due to the weak van der Waals interaction between the layers,
the energy differences among the lateral positions are very small,
as shown in table~\ref{tab:S1.1}.

The results show that the lowest-energy lateral position for the Graphene-\ce{BC3} system is the `Hollow'' stacking configuration.
As shown in figure~\ref{fig1.3},
each atom in \ce{BC3} projects to the center of a hexagon in graphene.
Both the Graphene-Borophene and Graphene-\ce{B4C3} bilayer systems favor the `Top'' stacking configuration.
In this arrangement, each atom-free hexagon in the bottom layer projects to the center of a hexagon in graphene.
However, the converse does not hold, because not every hexagon center in graphene aligns with an atom-free hexagon in the bottom layer, as illustrated in figure~\ref{fig1.3}.

Among all heterostructure systems, \ce{B4C3} shows the largest tensile strain of \qty{3.33}{\percent}.

Therefore, the relative coordination of the two monolayers directly influences the interfacial electronic structure.
The top and bottom views of the Graphene-\ce{B4C3} heterostructure reveal a clear threefold rotational symmetry in the upper and lower plots of the rightmost panel.

\subsubsection{Schottky barrier height}

Bilayer heterostructures composed of graphene and boron-carbide monolayers can function as Schottky diodes.
A high Schottky barrier blocks the reverse current from the metal to the semiconductor and enables the rectification effect of a diode.
Based on the Schottky--Mott model shown in figure~\ref{fig1.8},
the Schottky barrier at a metal--semiconductor interface~\cite{guo2017all} is determined by the relative positions of the Fermi level and the semiconductor band edges.

For $n$-type materials, the Schottky barrier $\Phi_n$ is defined as the energy difference between the conduction band minimum $E_C$ and the Fermi level $E_F$:

\begin{equation}
    \Phi_n = E_C - E_F
\end{equation}

Similarly, for $p$-type materials, the Schottky barrier $\Phi_p$ is defined as the energy difference between the Fermi level $E_F$ and the valence band maximum $E_V$:

\begin{equation}
    \Phi_p = E_F - E_V
\end{equation}

The sum of these two Schottky barriers is approximately equal to the semiconductor band gap $E_g$:

\begin{equation}
    \Phi_n + \Phi_p \approx E_g
\end{equation}

For the Graphene-\ce{B4C3} system illustrated in figure~\ref{fig1.8}, we find $\Phi_n = \qty{1.42}{\electronvolt}$ ($\qty{1.07}{\electronvolt}$) and $\Phi_p = \qty{0.87}{\electronvolt}$ ($\qty{0.55}{\electronvolt}$) using HSE06 (PBE).
Since $\Phi_p < \Phi_n$, this heterojunction forms a $p$-type Schottky barrier.
For the Graphene-\ce{BC3} heterostructure bilayer, we find $\Phi_n = \qty{0.19}{\electronvolt}$ ($\qty{0.003}{\electronvolt}$) and $\Phi_p = \qty{1.42}{\electronvolt}$ ($\qty{0.39}{\electronvolt}$) using HSE06 (PBE).
This result indicates that the Graphene-\ce{BC3} heterojunction forms an $n$-type Schottky barrier.
For device applications, a smaller Schottky barrier height, or even an Ohmic contact, is preferred to reduce contact resistance.

\subsection{Dielectric function}

Having determined the atomic and electronic properties of the bilayer systems,
we calculate the real part $\varepsilon_1$ and imaginary part $\varepsilon_2$ of the dielectric function,
from which the other optical properties can be determined, as given by equations~\ref{absorption_coefficient_from_dielectric_function},
~\ref{refractive_index_from_dielectric_response},
~\ref{extinction_coefficient_from_dielectric_response},
~\ref{reflectivity_from_refractive_index_and_extinction_coefficient},
and~\ref{energy_loss_spectrum_from_inverse_dielectric_function}.
The real part $\varepsilon_1$ measures the strength of the dynamical screening effects arising from charge excitations,
whereas the imaginary part $\varepsilon_2$ measures light absorption as a consequence of neutral and plasmonic charge excitations.
A negative value of the real part of the dielectric function indicates metallic character in the corresponding energy range of the electromagnetic spectrum.

It is first important to establish the appropriate $\vec{k}$-point set to yield converged results.
For graphene, we test a number of $\vec{k}$-point meshes and find that a $129 \times 129 \times 1$ mesh gives converged results, as shown in figure~\ref{S1.12}, in good agreement with previous results~\cite{warmbier2016}.
We therefore use this $\vec{k}$-point sampling density for the other single monolayers, whose unit cells are $2 \times 2$ relative to graphene, namely a $65 \times 65 \times 1$ $\vec{k}$-point mesh.
The dielectric functions for all the monolayers are shown in figure~\ref{S1.13}, where the results are consistent with previous calculations, namely graphene~\cite{warmbier2016}, \ce{BC3}~\cite{chen2019plasmon,mortazavi2019outstanding}, \ce{B4C3}~\cite{chang2019b4c3}, and borophene~\cite{huang2017,li2017first}.

The anisotropic in-plane and out-of-plane components of the dielectric function show that the optical response depends strongly on the polarization direction of the incoming light.
For the in-plane direction, the well-known $\pi$ plasmon of graphene emerges around \qty{4}{\electronvolt},
and the $\pi-\sigma$ plasmon emerges around \qty{14}{\electronvolt}.
The other monolayer systems also have a $\pi$-like plasmon,
which emerges for \ce{B4C3} around \qty{3}{\electronvolt},
for \ce{BC3} around \qty{2}{\electronvolt},
and for borophene around \qty{1}{\electronvolt}.
It can furthermore be seen from figure~\ref{S1.13} that, for the real part of the dielectric function in the out-of-plane direction for borophene,
a plasmon emerges at \qty{9.8}{\electronvolt}, as evidenced by the negative value that becomes positive around \qty{10.5}{\electronvolt}.

As previously reported, the latter three monolayers exhibit visible-light absorption,
which is also indicated by the in-plane imaginary part of the dielectric function shown in figure~\ref{S1.13}.
The static dielectric constants, namely the real part of the dielectric function at zero energy,
for \ce{BC3} are 4.0 for in-plane polarization and 1.4 for out-of-plane polarization as obtained using PBE.
These values can be compared with previously reported PBE values of 4.86 for in-plane polarization and 1.59 for out-of-plane polarization~\cite{mortazavi2019outstanding}.

For \ce{B4C3}, the in-plane static dielectric constant is 3.9,
and the out-of-plane value is calculated to be 1.5.
We note that these values reflect the combined monolayer-vacuum system.
Figure~\ref{S1.14} shows the corresponding optical properties for the monolayer systems.

Before presenting the results for the three heterostructure bilayer systems,
we first perform a convergence check for the number of unoccupied bands included in the calculation of the dielectric function.
Figure~\ref{S1.15} shows that 128 bands yield well-converged results for the representative Graphene-\ce{B4C3} system,
and we therefore use this value for all three bilayer calculations.
Further tests of the energy convergence criterion are reported in figure~\ref{S1.16}.

% Graphene-Borophene
The results for the dielectric function of the Graphene-Borophene heterojunction are shown in figure~\ref{fig1.9},
as obtained using both the PBE and HSE06 functionals.
The corresponding full dielectric tensor components are provided in figure~\ref{S1.18}.
Compared with the PBE results,
HSE06 yields a blue shift of the features to higher energies.
The real part exhibits characteristics of both graphene and borophene,
with plasmons emerging around the same energies as those of the respective monolayers shown in figure~\ref{S1.13},
namely around \qty{5}{\electronvolt} (\qty{4}{\electronvolt}) and \qty{2}{\electronvolt} (\qty{1}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE), respectively.
There is, however, a significant difference in the out-of-plane dielectric function compared with the monolayer systems.
The borophene-related plasmon that emerges at around \qty{9.8}{\electronvolt} in the monolayer shifts to higher energies and now appears at around \qty{17}{\electronvolt} (\qty{14}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE),
with a plasmon resonance frequency at about \qty{18}{\electronvolt} (\qty{15}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE),
as determined by the energy at which the real part of the dielectric function changes from negative to positive.
In addition, a new feature with two peaks occurs around \qtyrange{2}{5}{\electronvolt} (\qtyrange{1}{4}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE) in both the real and imaginary parts of the dielectric function.
This feature in the imaginary part is attributed to interband transitions involving borophene- and graphene-derived states around the $\mathrm{K}$ point and along the direction towards the $\mathrm{M}$ and $\Gamma$ points.
A similar feature appears for the other bilayer heterojunctions, although it shows only one main peak.
For in-plane polarization, the first main peak of $\varepsilon_2$ occurs in the visible range and is related to $\pi-\pi^*$ transitions.
The main peaks along the out-of-plane direction are broad and occur in the energy range \qtyrange{9}{18.5}{\electronvolt} (\qtyrange{8}{17.5}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE),
being related to $\pi-\sigma^*$ and $\pi^*-\sigma$ transitions.

% Graphene-BC3
The results for the dielectric function of the Graphene-\ce{BC3} heterostructure are shown in figure~\ref{fig1.10},
as obtained using both the PBE and HSE06 functionals.
The corresponding full dielectric tensor components are provided in figure~\ref{S1.17}.
Interestingly, and in contrast to Graphene-Borophene,
the dielectric tensor for this system contains complex non-zero off-diagonal components.
These components satisfy the antisymmetric relations
$\varepsilon_{xy}=-\varepsilon_{yx}$,
$\varepsilon_{xz}=-\varepsilon_{zx}$,
and $\varepsilon_{yz}=-\varepsilon_{zy}$.
The dielectric tensor describes the response of a material to an external electric field,
or equivalently, how the polarization of the material changes under the applied electric field.
For an incident photon,
the tensor components describe the response of the material to the photon electric-field component along different directions.
For example, the elements $\varepsilon_{xy}$ and $\varepsilon_{yx}$ describe the coupling between the $x$ and $y$ directions,
namely the response to an electric field applied along one direction resulting in polarization along the other direction, and vice versa.
However, these off-diagonal components are mainly non-zero only in the low-energy region and remain small in magnitude.
Therefore, the following discussion focuses on the in-plane and out-of-plane diagonal components,
which describe the dominant optical response for the polarization directions considered here.

In contrast to the Graphene-Borophene system,
the real part of the dielectric function is positive over the whole energy range considered.
There are, however, energy ranges where it approaches zero,
namely in the region \qtyrange{2.5}{5}{\electronvolt} and from \qty{13}{\electronvolt} (\qty{15}{\electronvolt}) onwards for PBE (HSE06).
When $\varepsilon_1$ is zero but not negative,
this suggests that the system is close to resonance but has not fully entered the regime where strong optical reflection or plasmonic modes are dominant,
since such modes typically require $\varepsilon_1 < 0$.
Another difference from the Graphene-Borophene system is the sizeable in-plane value of both $\varepsilon_1$ and $\varepsilon_2$ in the region \qtyrange{10}{15}{\electronvolt}.
Similar to the Graphene-Borophene system, however,
the out-of-plane real part of the dielectric function becomes negative at around \qtyrange{16}{17}{\electronvolt} (\qtyrange{14}{15}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE),
indicating the presence of a plasmon.
However, monolayer \ce{BC3} does not exhibit this feature, unlike borophene, as shown in figure~\ref{S1.13}.
It is therefore presumably related to the $\pi-\sigma$-type plasmon present for graphene at approximately \qty{15}{\electronvolt},
but involves transitions between states associated with both monolayers.
For in-plane polarization, the first main peaks of $\varepsilon_2$ occur in the visible range and are related to $\pi-\pi^*$ transitions.
The main peaks along the out-of-plane direction are broad and occur in the energy range \qtyrange{11}{17}{\electronvolt} (\qtyrange{9}{16}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE),
being related to $\pi-\sigma^*$ and $\pi^*-\sigma$ transitions.

% Graphene-B4C3
The results for the dielectric function of the Graphene-\ce{B4C3} heterostructure are shown in figure~\ref{fig1.11},
as obtained using both the PBE and HSE06 functionals.
The corresponding full dielectric tensor components are provided in figure~\ref{S1.19}.
The off-diagonal $\varepsilon_{xy}$ component is also included in figure~\ref{fig1.11}.
The real part of the in-plane dielectric function is negative in two regions:
\qtyrange{5}{6}{\electronvolt} for HSE06 (\qtyrange{4}{5}{\electronvolt} for PBE)
and \qtyrange{17}{18}{\electronvolt} for HSE06 (\qtyrange{15}{16}{\electronvolt} for PBE).
These negative regions indicate the presence of plasmons in the ultraviolet region.
The out-of-plane component also displays negative values at about \qtyrange{17}{18}{\electronvolt} for HSE06 (\qtyrange{14.5}{15.5}{\electronvolt} for PBE).
Similar to Graphene-\ce{BC3},
the first main peaks of $\varepsilon_2$ for in-plane polarization are also in the visible range.
The broad main peaks along the out-of-plane direction occur in the energy range \qtyrange{10}{18}{\electronvolt} (\qtyrange{9}{17}{\electronvolt}) for HSE06 (PBE).

The dielectric tensor for this system also contains complex non-zero off-diagonal components.
These components satisfy the antisymmetric relations
$\varepsilon_{xy}=-\varepsilon_{yx}$,
$\varepsilon_{xz}=-\varepsilon_{zx}$,
and $\varepsilon_{yz}=-\varepsilon_{zy}$, as shown in figure~\ref{S1.19}.
In contrast to Graphene-\ce{BC3}, however, these components remain non-zero over a considerable energy range,
namely from \qtyrange{0}{5}{\electronvolt} and from \qtyrange{10}{15}{\electronvolt}.
This behaviour is consistent with the lower symmetry of Graphene-\ce{B4C3}.
The system has a $\mathrm{P}3$ space group,
with threefold rotational symmetry along the $c$ axis, corresponding to the $z$ direction.
It also lacks mirror-symmetry planes perpendicular to this axis or within the $a,b$ plane,
corresponding to the $x,y$ plane.
This lower symmetry allows the presence of off-diagonal elements in the dielectric tensor.
Therefore, the dielectric response of this material can exhibit anisotropic coupling between different crystallographic directions.
The presence of complex off-diagonal elements in the dielectric tensor of a material with the $\mathrm{P}3$ space group indicates anisotropic and possibly chiral optical properties,
which could lead to effects such as optical activity.
It also indicates that Graphene-\ce{B4C3} dissipates energy differently depending on the direction of the electric field.

\subsection{Derived optical properties}

To further examine the optical properties of the heterostructure bilayers,
we calculate the absorption coefficient, energy-loss spectrum, reflectivity, refractive index, and extinction coefficient.

Figure~\ref{fig1.12} shows the HSE06 absorption coefficients and energy-loss spectra of the three systems for in-plane and out-of-plane polarizations.
The corresponding component-resolved PBE and HSE06 results are provided in figures~\ref{S1.20}--\ref{S1.24}.

The absorption threshold corresponds to the first peak of $\varepsilon_2(\omega)$.
For all systems, the absorption coefficient starts from a very small value and increases with photon energy,
consistent with the lowest direct transition energies in the band structure.
All three systems absorb in the visible region, \qtyrange{1.7}{3.3}{\electronvolt}.
The Graphene-Borophene heterostructure shows a significantly larger absorption coefficient than the other two systems,
with a sharp rise around \qty{1}{\electronvolt}.
This feature can be attributed to transitions between the flat valence-band region around the $\Gamma$ point and the low-lying conduction band in the same region of the Brillouin zone.
The Graphene-\ce{BC3} heterostructure shows a similar sharp rise at a slightly higher energy, around \qty{2.5}{\electronvolt},
followed by Graphene-\ce{B4C3} at around \qty{3.5}{\electronvolt}.
These latter features arise from transitions between relatively flat valence and conduction bands along the $\Gamma$--$\mathrm{M}$ direction.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.80\linewidth]{figures_proj1/proj1.12a.pdf}
    \par\nointerlineskip
    \includegraphics[width=0.80\linewidth]{figures_proj1/proj1.12b.pdf}
\caption{In-plane and out-of-plane absorption coefficients and energy-loss spectra of the three heterostructure bilayers.}
\label{fig1.12}
\end{figure}

The energy-loss spectrum in figure~\ref{fig1.12} shows a sharp low-energy feature for Graphene-\ce{BC3} and Graphene-\ce{B4C3},
which is absent for Graphene-Borophene.
This feature is related to Dirac-like plasmons.
The broader features between \qtyrange{3}{8}{\electronvolt} can be attributed to $\pi$-like plasmons.
Individual interband transitions presumably coexist with these plasmons and form a broad background.
When interband transitions and plasmons occur in the same energy range,
the two types of excitations can couple,
giving finite plasmon lifetimes as well as interband-transition character.
The features around \qty{15}{\electronvolt}, where a sharp increase begins, may be associated with $\pi-\sigma$-type plasmons.
This assignment is consistent with the negative real part of the out-of-plane dielectric-function component,
which indicates a plasmon around this energy for all three bilayer heterostructures.

Figure~\ref{fig1.13} shows the reflectivity and refractive index of the three heterostructures,
as obtained using the HSE06 functional for in-plane and out-of-plane polarizations.
The refractive index is related to the real and imaginary parts of the dielectric function.
Its trend is similar to that of $\varepsilon_1$,
indicating that the real part of the dielectric function plays the leading role.
The refractive index determines how much the path of light is bent, or refracted, when light enters a material.
For visible light, most transparent materials have refractive indices between 1 and 2.
The absolute refractive index of an optical medium is defined as the ratio between the speed of light in vacuum and the phase velocity of light in the material,
where the phase velocity is the speed at which the crests of the light wave propagate.
Therefore, if the phase velocity is larger than the speed of light in vacuum,
the refractive index can be smaller than 1.
For frequencies near the plasmon resonance in 2D materials,
the refractive index can drop below 1 due to the strong interaction between light and plasmonic oscillations.

\centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.80\linewidth]{figures_proj1/proj1.13a.pdf}
    \par\nointerlineskip
    \includegraphics[width=0.80\linewidth]{figures_proj1/proj1.13b.pdf}
\caption{In-plane and out-of-plane reflectivity and refractive index of the three heterostructure bilayers.}
\label{fig1.13}
\end{figure}

For in-plane polarization,
the refractive index is close to, or smaller than, 1 in the energy range of approximately \qtyrange{5}{7}{\electronvolt}
and above approximately \qty{17}{\electronvolt} for all three systems.
For out-of-plane polarization,
the refractive index is close to, or smaller than, 1 for energies above about \qty{14}{\electronvolt}.
In these energy ranges,
the structures are highly transparent.
These regions also correspond to the energies where plasmons occur.

Figure~\ref{fig1.14} shows the HSE06 extinction coefficient for in-plane and out-of-plane polarizations.
For out-of-plane polarization,
it displays a broad maximum from \qtyrange{0}{13}{\electronvolt}.
For in-plane polarization,
the maximum occurs in the regions \qtyrange{0}{2}{\electronvolt},
\qtyrange{0}{2.5}{\electronvolt},
and \qtyrange{0}{3}{\electronvolt}
for the Graphene-Borophene, Graphene-\ce{BC3}, and Graphene-\ce{B4C3} heterostructures, respectively.
A broad peak also appears in the region around \qtyrange{7}{15}{\electronvolt}.
These energy regions therefore correspond to strong photon absorption.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{1_conclusions}

In the present work,
we investigated the atomic, electronic, and optical properties of Graphene-Borophene,
Graphene-\ce{BC3}, and Graphene-\ce{B4C3} bilayer heterostructure systems
using first-principles calculations with the HSE06 and PBE functionals.
We find that all systems have small binding energies between the layers,
indicating that graphene interacts weakly with the monolayers through van der Waals interactions.
The Graphene-Borophene bilayer is metallic,
whereas the Graphene-\ce{BC3} and Graphene-\ce{B4C3} structures maintain the characteristic linear bands of graphene around the $\mathrm{K}$ point,
with only a very small band-gap opening of \qtyrange{20}{50}{\milli\electronvolt}.
The calculated optical properties show absorption in the visible region of the spectrum for all heterostructures,
with Graphene-Borophene exhibiting the strongest absorption.
All heterostructures support plasmons.
For Graphene-Borophene,
two low-energy plasmons are observed,
one emerging at \qty{2}{\electronvolt} and the other around \qty{5}{\electronvolt}.
The Graphene-\ce{B4C3} bilayer heterostructure has non-negligible off-diagonal elements in the dielectric tensor over a range of energies,
suggesting that it could exhibit optically active, possibly chiral, effects.

Figures~\ref{S1.17}--\ref{S1.19} show the full dielectric-function tensor components of the three bilayer systems,
as calculated using both the PBE and HSE06 functionals.
The diagonal components describe the dominant in-plane and out-of-plane optical responses,
whereas the off-diagonal components describe the coupling between different Cartesian directions.
These off-diagonal components are negligible for Graphene-Borophene,
but remain finite for Graphene-\ce{BC3} and Graphene-\ce{B4C3}.
They satisfy the antisymmetric relations
$\varepsilon_{yx}=-\varepsilon_{xy}$,
$\varepsilon_{yz}=-\varepsilon_{zy}$,
and $\varepsilon_{zx}=-\varepsilon_{xz}$.

In addition, figures~\ref{S1.20}--\ref{S1.24} show the optical properties derived from the dielectric function for the three bilayer heterostructures,
also calculated using both the PBE and HSE06 functionals.
These properties include the absorption coefficient, energy-loss spectrum, reflectivity, refractive index, and extinction coefficient.
