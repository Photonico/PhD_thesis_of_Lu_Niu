\chapter[Functional Properties of Penta-Bipyramid Boron Phases]{Superconductivity, Magnetism, and Directional Plasmonic Modes in Two-Dimensional Penta-Bipyramid Boron Allotropes}
\label{cha:project_boron}

\section{Abstract}
\label{2_abstract}

Bulk orthorhombic boron, o-\ce{B14}, represents an intriguing, recently predicted,
three-dimensional boron allotrope.
Its structure consists of edge-sharing pentagonal bipyramids
with diverse bond lengths.
These structural motifs facilitate the formation of unusual seven-centre-two-electron $\pi$-bonds,
which were predicted for the first time in three-dimensional boron allotropes.
The presence of these bonds highlights the complexity and versatility of boron chemistry under ambient conditions.
Moreover, this material is predicted to be a superconductor with a critical temperature $T_c$ of \qty{29.1}{K}~\cite{han2023superconducting}.

In this chapter, we investigate the electronic and optical properties of bulk o-\ce{B14},
its monolayer and bilayer forms,
and their hydrogen-terminated counterparts using first-principles calculations.
The pristine monolayer and bilayer, as well as the hydrogen-terminated bilayer,
are predicted to remain dynamically stable and to exhibit a remarkable variety of electronic behaviour,
including semiconductivity with a band-gap of \qty{0.67}{\electronvolt} in the bilayer,
superconductivity with a $T_c$ of \qty{24.3}{K} in the hydrogen-terminated bilayer,
and magnetism in the monolayer.
The directional bonding network drives anisotropic plasmonic and optical responses,
as well as absorption in the visible region of the spectrum.
These properties suggest promising opportunities for applications in advanced optoelectronic, plasmonic and quantum devices.

\section{Introduction}
\label{2_introduction}

Elemental boron possesses a unique combination of light weight, exceptional hardness, and thermal and chemical stability.
Due to these properties, it has potential for high-technology applications such as catalysis, sensors, energy storage, piezoelectrics, and nanodevices~\cite{ranjan2024properties}.
Boron exists in multiple allotropes with complex crystal structures arising from its electron-deficient bonding and small atomic size.
The most well-characterized and stable crystalline forms are $\alpha$-rhombohedral boron, $\alpha$-B with 12 atoms per unit cell;
$\beta$-rhombohedral boron, $\beta$-B with 105--108 atoms per unit cell~\cite{widom2008symmetry};
and $\gamma$-orthorhombic boron, $\gamma$-B with 28 atoms per unit cell~\cite{an2016new}.
$\alpha$-B consists of \ce{B12} icosahedral building blocks and forms at low temperature,
whereas $\beta$-B has the same structural motif, but also includes interstitial boron atoms.
$\beta$-B forms at room temperature and is the most stable structure.
$\gamma$-B is also composed of \ce{B12} icosahedral units and forms at high pressure and
temperature.
The structures are predominantly characterized by covalent bonding,
but the electron deficiency of boron gives rise to multi-centre bonding,
such as three-centre-two-electron bonds.
Additionally, boron-rich compounds exhibit promising semiconducting and thermoelectric properties,
with potential use in next-generation electronics and energy-harvesting devices.

Generally, the superconducting critical temperature $T_c$ of elemental superconductors is relatively low at ambient pressure.
A representative elemental superconductor, \ce{Nb}, has a $T_c$ of about \qty{9}{K}.
Under high pressure, the $T_c$ can be significantly increased compared with the ambient-pressure value,
but high pressure hinders experimental studies and practical applications.
Therefore,
it is important to explore elemental superconductors with a high $T_c$ under low or ambient pressure.
Interestingly, superconductivity with a $T_c$ of \qty{35}{K} has been reported in a three-dimensional kagome-like boron allotrope under \qty{7}{GPa} on the basis of \textit{ab initio} calculations~\cite{ouyang2025superconductivity},
while earlier work predicts a $T_c$ exceeding \qty{15}{K} in three-dimensional boron allotropes~\cite{zhang2020superconducting}.

Boron can also form isolated clusters which exhibit many intriguing properties,
including fluxionality~\cite{pan2019fluxional}.
Fluxionality describes the ability to undergo continuous structural rearrangements without breaking chemical bonds in a way that permanently alters the system.
Recent studies have focused on two-dimensional forms of boron,
including monolayer borophene, where many different structures have been observed.
Recent review papers have summarized these developments:
Wang \textit{et al.}~\cite{wang2024borophene} have summarized the synthesis and properties of borophene,
while Mishra \textit{et al.}~\cite{mishra2024borophene} have reviewed the potential of borophene in various technological fields such as batteries, supercapacitors, and fuel cells, as well as the associated synthesis techniques.
Other recent reviews of borophene focus on experimental aspects~\cite{mohanty2023review,yang2023borophenes,kumar2024,xie2020two},
and provide a survey of structures starting from the bulk allotropes, boron clusters,
potential building blocks for two-dimensional boron crystals, and two-dimensional few-layered boron sheets~\cite{sun2017two}.
As a light two-dimensional material with good flexibility,
borophene has potential applications in flexible and wearable devices, biomedicines,
and biosensors~\cite{mozvashi2021mechanical,zhang2017elasticity}.

From the theoretical side,
two-dimensional boron has also attracted significant recent interest due to its complex electronic structure,
which can give rise to predicted semiconducting behaviour~\cite{qureshi2024bilayer},
including in twisted bilayer borophene~\cite{jin2022tuning},
superconductivity~\cite{gao2017prediction,han2023superconducting},
magnetism~\cite{zhu2019magnetic}, and topological states~\cite{wang2025second,rakshit2025exploring,wang2019serendipity}.
Furthermore, the $\beta_{12}$ sheet has been found to host gapless Dirac cones~\cite{feng2017dirac},
and a bilayer kagome structure has been predicted to host multiple van Hove singularities~\cite{gao2024bilayer}.
Half-metallic ferromagnetism has also been reported in a two-dimensional kagome lattice composed of a triangular arrangement of boron atoms within triangles~\cite{kim2017boron}.

Synthesizing borophene is notably challenging due to several intrinsic and extrinsic factors.
Unlike graphene, borophene lacks a naturally occurring layered bulk counterpart,
which eliminates the possibility of exfoliation-based synthesis.
Its high chemical reactivity,
especially with oxygen and moisture, makes it unstable under ambient conditions,
requiring ultra-high vacuum environments for fabrication and handling.
These factors collectively make the reproducible and scalable synthesis of monolayer borophene a significant materials science hurdle.
To overcome the challenges of synthesizing monolayer borophene,
researchers have focused on carefully controlled epitaxial growth techniques such as molecular beam epitaxy on inert metal substrates~\cite{chen2022synthesis}.
Another study has achieved growth of $\beta_{12}$- and $\alpha$-sheets of borophene on \ce{Ag}(111) by direct evaporation of a pure boron source in an e-beam evaporator~\cite{zhong2017metastable}.

Substrates stabilize the two-dimensional structure of borophene during growth by promoting specific polymorphs through surface templating.
Maintaining ultra-high vacuum conditions during synthesis helps prevent oxidation and contamination.
Another strategy to enhance stability uses hydrogen termination,
which passivates the highly reactive surface boron atoms.
This passivation can reduce the tendency to oxidize or react with ambient species,
thereby improving chemical stability in air.
Moreover, hydrogen termination can modify the electronic properties,
potentially opening a band-gap or altering metallic behaviour,
which may be advantageous for specific applications~\cite{hou2020ultrastable,li2021synthesis}.
Large-scale synthesis of freestanding atomic sheets of borophene has been reported through a novel liquid-phase exfoliation approach,
where electron microscopy confirmed the presence of the $\beta_{12}$ and $\chi_{3}$ phases,
as well as their intermediate phases~\cite{ranjan2019freestanding}.

Different from flat borophenes,
a novel highly stable two-dimensional boron structure has been recently predicted on the basis of \textit{ab initio} calculations.
This structure is composed of \ce{B12} clusters linked by single B-B bonds and is a narrow band-gap semiconductor~\cite{zhang2023novel}.
The optical properties of two-dimensional allotropes have also been investigated.
It has been reported that the plasmon frequencies reach the near infrared and even visible regions,
making borophene among the first materials with two-dimensional plasmons at such high energies without doping~\cite{huang2017}.

Recently, a bulk orthorhombic boron allotrope, o-\ce{B14},
with interesting properties was predicted~\cite{han2023superconducting}.
Its structure is characterized by edge-sharing pentagonal bipyramids with varying B-B bond lengths ranging from \qtyrange{1.644}{1.966}{\angstrom}.
These structural motifs facilitate the formation of peculiar seven-centre-two-electron $\pi$-bonds,
which were predicted for the first time in three-dimensional boron allotropes.
Remarkably, this material is predicted to be a superconductor with a transition temperature of \qty{29.1}{K}.

This orthorhombic o-\ce{B14} structure is a particularly promising candidate for further investigation,
as it introduces the pentagonal \ce{B7} bipyramidal motif as a fundamental structural unit in crystalline boron,
which differs markedly from the \ce{B12} icosahedral units that dominate most known boron phases.
This unusual topology leads to highly delocalized multicentre bonding,
including the predicted seven-centre-two-electron $\pi$ bonds,
suggesting that the electronic structure may host unconventional features such as anisotropic transport, unusual phonon behaviour,
and potentially strong electron-phonon coupling.
Since the phase is predicted to be energetically competitive and dynamically stable at ambient pressure, \textit{ab initio} studies of
its electronic band structure, phonon spectrum, and superconducting properties would be valuable,
as would studies that explore how the unique bipyramidal building blocks influence the broader physics of electron-deficient boron networks.
Moreover, this also raises the question of whether it may be stable in two-dimensional form,
where the interlayer connectivity consists only of single B-B bonds.

In this chapter, we use density functional theory calculations to investigate the electronic and optical properties of bulk o-\ce{B14} and its monolayer and bilayer forms.
Hydrogen termination of the two-dimensional structures is also considered.
Interestingly, bilayer o-\ce{B14} is found to be a semiconductor with a band-gap of around
\qty{0.67}{\electronvolt} as calculated using the HSE06 hybrid functional.
The hydrogen-terminated bilayer, however, is predicted to be superconducting with a $T_c$ of \qty{24.3}{K},
while monolayer o-\ce{B14} is found to be magnetic.
The optical properties reveal distinctive anisotropic behaviour and the existence of plasmons in the near-infrared to mid-infrared range for in-plane excitation,
as well as the ultraviolet energy range for out-of-plane excitation,
broadening the potential of boron for applications in optoelectronics, plasmonics, and catalysis.

This chapter is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{2_methods} presents the computational methods.
Then, section~\ref{2_results} discusses the electronic, superconducting, and optical properties.
Finally, section~\ref{2_conclusions} gives the conclusions.

\section{Computation methods}
\label{2_methods}

The density functional theory calculations are performed using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package, VASP~\cite{kresse1993ab,kresse1994ab,kresse1996efficiency,kresse1996efficient}.
The projector augmented wave method is used to describe the electron-ion interactions~\cite{kresse1999ultrasoft}.
The generalized gradient approximation of Perdew--Burke--Ernzerhof is employed for the exchange-correlation functional~\cite{perdew1996generalized},
along with the D3 dispersion correction with Becke--Johnson damping to account for long-range van der Waals interactions~\cite{becke2005density,grimme2010consistent,grimme2011effect}.
For bulk o-\ce{B14}, a $\Gamma$-centered k-point mesh of $34 \times 48 \times 31$ is used with a plane-wave energy cutoff of \qty{600}{\electronvolt}.
The electronic convergence criterion is set to \qty{1e-6}{\electronvolt},
and the structures are relaxed until all forces on each atom are below \qty{0.01}{\electronvolt\per\angstrom}.
For the two-dimensional systems, a vacuum region of approximately \qty{22}{\angstrom} is included,
and a k-point mesh of $20 \times 18 \times 1$ is used.
Convergence tests show that these settings yield accurate results,
as shown in figures~\ref{S2.1}--\ref{S2.3} of the Supplementary Information.

For selected calculations,
we also use the screened hybrid exchange-correlation functional of Heyd, Scuseria, and Ernzerhof, HSE06,
which gives more accurate band-gap energies~\cite{sassnick2021electronic,krukau2006influence}.
Unless explicitly stated otherwise,
all figures show results obtained using the Perdew--Burke--Ernzerhof functional.
For the density-of-states calculations for bulk o-\ce{B14},
we use a $\Gamma$-centered k-point mesh of $20 \times 28 \times 18$.
For the two-dimensional systems,
we use a mesh of $20 \times 18 \times 1$.
The Fermi-level smearing is calculated using the Gaussian method with a broadening value of \qty{0.1}{\electronvolt}.

For hydrogen termination,
the hydrogen atoms are placed on top of the boron atoms of the bipyramids.
The hollow-bridge site,
located between the two apical boron atoms on each side of the layer,
is also considered for comparison,
as shown in figure~\ref{S2.7}.

The adsorption energy per atom, $E_{\mathrm{ad}}$, of hydrogen atoms on the surface is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{ad}} = \frac{E_{\mathrm{H/B}} - E_{\mathrm{B}} - E_{\mathrm{H}}}{n_{\mathrm{H}}}
\label{hydrogen_adsorption_energy}
\end{equation}

here, $E_{\mathrm{H/B}}$ is the total energy of the adsorption system,
$E_{\mathrm{B}}$ represents the total energy of the pristine two-dimensional boron structure,
$E_{\mathrm{H}}$ denotes the total energy of a hydrogen atom in the \ce{H2} molecule,
and $n_{\mathrm{H}}$ denotes the number of hydrogen atoms in the unit cell.

The cohesive energy per atom, $E_{\mathrm{c}}$, of the boron systems is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{c}}
    = \frac{E_{\mathrm{B\text{-}struc}} - n_{\mathrm{B}}E_{\mathrm{B\text{-}atom}}}{n_{\mathrm{B}}}
\label{cohesive_energy_per_atom}
\end{equation}

here,
$E_{\mathrm{B\text{-}struc}}$ represents the total energy of the boron structure of interest,
$E_{\mathrm{B\text{-}atom}}$ is the total energy of a free boron atom calculated in a large supercell,
and $n_{\mathrm{B}}$ denotes the number of boron atoms in the unit cell of the structure.

The charge density difference, $\Delta \rho(\vec{r}\,)$, for hydrogen adsorption on the surface is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta \rho(\vec{r}\,) =
    \rho_{\mathrm{H/B}}(\vec{r}\,)
    - \rho_{\mathrm{B}}(\vec{r}\,)
    - \rho_{\mathrm{H}}(\vec{r}\,)
\label{charge_density_difference_hydrogen_adsorption}
\end{equation}

here, $\rho_{\mathrm{H/B}}(\vec{r}\,)$ is the total charge density of the adsorbed system,
and $\rho_{\mathrm{B}}(\vec{r}\,)$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{H}}(\vec{r}\,)$ are the total charge densities of the clean boron structure and the hydrogen atom,
respectively.

The dynamical stability of the pristine monolayer and bilayer,
as well as the hydrogen-terminated structures,
is examined from the phonon dispersion curves using the finite-difference method.
The main phonon dispersions are calculated using $(3\times3)$ supercells.
The comparison with $(2\times2)$ supercells is included in figures~\ref{S2.8} and~\ref{S2.9}.
For the hydrogen-terminated bilayer,
\textit{ab initio} molecular dynamics calculations are also carried out at a temperature of \qty{400}{K} using VASP,
as shown in figure~\ref{S2.10}.

The superconducting behaviour of o-\ce{B14} is investigated systematically in three structural phases,
namely bulk, monolayer, and hydrogen-terminated bilayer.
Calculations are carried out using the Perdew--Burke--Ernzerhof formulation of the generalized gradient approximation~\cite{perdew1996generalized},
as provided in Quantum ESPRESSO~\cite{giannozzi2009quantum}.
Norm-conserving pseudopotentials and a kinetic energy cutoff of $45$~Ry are chosen~\cite{hamann2013optimized},
and a vacuum spacing of \qty{15}{\angstrom} is added for the two-dimensional systems to suppress spurious periodic interactions.
Monkhorst--Pack k-point meshes of $9 \times 12 \times 8$ for the bulk,
$10 \times 10 \times 1$ for the monolayer,
and $9 \times 9 \times 1$ for the bilayer are employed to sample the Brillouin zone~\cite{methfessel1989high}.
The phonon dispersions are calculated on $\vec{q}$-point grids of $3 \times 4 \times 3$,
$5 \times 5 \times 1$, and $3 \times 3 \times 1$, respectively~\cite{baroni2001phonons}.
Atomic positions and lattice parameters are relaxed until residual forces are below $1\times10^{-5}$~Ry/bohr,
corresponding to \qty{2.6e-4}{\electronvolt\per\angstrom}.

Maximally localized Wannier functions are constructed in Wannier90 by initializing projections on boron $s$ and $p$ orbitals~\cite{pizzi2020wannier90}.
Uniform k-point meshes of $6 \times 8 \times 6$ for the bulk and $6 \times 6 \times 1$ for the monolayer and bilayer are used in Wannier interpolation.
Superconductivity is subsequently analysed using the EPW code~\cite{giannozzi2009quantum,giannozzi2020quantum,giannozzi2017advanced,ponce2016epw},
which is employed to evaluate the electron-phonon coupling strength and estimate the Allen--Dynes transition temperatures, $T_c$~\cite{margine2013anisotropic}.
The superconducting properties are also obtained by solving the fully anisotropic Migdal--Eliashberg equations~\cite{bardeen1955electron,giustino2017electron,margine2013anisotropic},
as implemented in the EPW code.
The Morel--Anderson pseudopotential $\mu^{*}$ is chosen to be $0.13$,
consistent with the typical semi-empirical range of $0.10$--$0.16$ used in Migdal--Eliashberg calculations for light-element and $sp$-bonded superconductors~\cite{allen-dynes},
and with the value adopted in the literature for boron-based two-dimensional superconductors~\cite{zhao2019two}.
With this choice,
the calculated electron-phonon coupling strengths, $\lambda$, and $T_c$ indicate clear dimensionality effects,
as discussed in Section~\ref{2_superconducting}.

For the calculation of the optical properties within VASP,
in particular the frequency-dependent dielectric tensor $\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}(\omega)$,
the k-point meshes of $20 \times 28 \times 18$ and $20 \times 18 \times 1$ are found to yield converged results for the bulk and two-dimensional systems,
respectively, as shown in figure~\ref{S2.4}.
A convergence check for the number of unoccupied bands included in the calculation of the dielectric function is also performed.
Figure~\ref{S2.5} shows the result for bulk o-\ce{B14}.
The result shows that $128$ bands yield well-converged results,
and this value is used for all systems.
The microscopic expression for the dielectric response,
the Kramers--Kronig relation,
and the derived optical quantities are introduced in Chapter~\ref{cha:fundamentals_b},
especially Sections~\ref{sec:from_electronic_response_to_dielectric_function} and~\ref{sec:from_dielectric_function_to_optical_properties}.

The dielectric tensor contains the combined dielectric response of the two-dimensional material and the vacuum,
so the magnitude of $\varepsilon_{1}(\omega)$ and $\varepsilon_{2}(\omega)$ depends linearly on the vacuum thickness~\cite{weng2018honeycomb,laturia2018dielectric}.
Therefore,
to obtain the intrinsic dielectric function of the structures,
the calculated dielectric functions are renormalized following Ref.~\cite{yang2021method}:

\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{2}^{\mathrm{2D}}(\omega)
    = \frac{d_{\mathrm{SC}}}{d_{\mathrm{2D}}}
    \varepsilon_{2}^{\mathrm{SC}}(\omega)
    \label{dielectric_vacuum_correction_imaginary}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{1}^{\mathrm{2D}}(\omega) =
    1 + \frac{d_{\mathrm{SC}}}{d_{\mathrm{2D}}}
    \left[\varepsilon_{1}^{\mathrm{SC}}(\omega)-1\right]
    \label{dielectric_vacuum_correction_real}
\end{equation}

here, $\varepsilon_{i}^{\mathrm{2D}}(\omega)$ and $\varepsilon_{i}^{\mathrm{SC}}(\omega)$,
with $i=1,2$, are the dielectric functions of the studied two-dimensional structure without the influence of the vacuum region and of the whole supercell,
respectively, and $d_{\mathrm{SC}}$ is the length of the supercell.
The thickness $d_{\mathrm{2D}}$ for the monolayer, bilayer,
and hydrogen-terminated bilayer is \qty{5.897}{\angstrom},
\qty{9.412}{\angstrom}, and \qty{11.209}{\angstrom}, respectively.
The thickness is defined in figure~\ref{S2.6}.
In the literature, this correction is often ignored in studies on two-dimensional materials,
which leads to errors in the reported dielectric functions and therefore the optical properties of the two-dimensional materials.
We note that interband transitions presumably coexist with the plasmons and form a broad background.
The coexistence of individual transitions and plasmons in the same energy range leads to coupling between the two types of excitations,
and hence to finite plasmon lifetimes in the presence of interband transitions~\cite{li2017first}.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Bulk o-\ce{B14}: atomic and electronic structure}{Bulk o-B14: atomic and electronic structure}}
\label{2_bulk_atomic_electronic_structure}

The structural and electronic properties of bulk o-\ce{B14} are investigated first.
The atomic structure is shown in figure~\ref{fig:ob14_atomic_structure}.
This boron allotrope has been predicted, on the basis of first-principles calculations,
to be dynamically and thermodynamically stable~\cite{han2023superconducting}.
The structural properties are listed in table~\ref{tab:bulk_ob14_structure}.
Following the previous work on this boron allotrope~\cite{han2023superconducting},
the boron atoms in the unit cell are assigned to three groups,
as indicated by the circles in panel~(c) of figure~\ref{fig:ob14_atomic_structure} and sketched in panel~(f).
These groups are denoted as G2, G3, and G7.
This grouping was obtained using the approach of solid-state adaptive natural density partitioning to analyse the multi-centre bonding.
This method combines ideas from Lewis theory and aromaticity.
It was found that, aside from the four normal two-centre-two-electron and the sixteen three-centre-two-electron B-B $\sigma$-bonds,
there are unusual seven-centre-two-electron $\pi$-bonds~\cite{han2023superconducting}.

The calculated band structure of bulk o-\ce{B14},
shown in the upper-left panel of figure~\ref{fig:bulk_ob14_band_dos},
exhibits metallic behaviour with three bands crossing the Fermi level.
The corresponding high-symmetry k-path is shown in the upper-right panel.
These bands include steep bands that promote high electron velocities,
as well as flat bands near the Fermi level that enhance the density of states and favour electronic interactions.
Such electronic features suggest the potential for unusual transport and quantum properties.
Furthermore,
the projected density of states shown in the lower panel
shows strong orbital hybridization between the boron $2p$ orbitals within the structural units,
which reinforces the stability of the bonding framework.
The calculated cohesive energy of bulk o-\ce{B14} is \qty{-6.16}{\electronvolt}.
Relative to the ground-state phase of boron, $\alpha$-\ce{B12},
the total energy of o-\ce{B14} is \qty{0.232}{\electronvolt} higher~\cite{han2023superconducting}.

\subsubsection{Dynamic stability: phonon dispersion}
\label{xene_ob14_phonon_dispersion}

To investigate whether the monolayer and bilayer structures,
both with and without hydrogen adsorption,
are dynamically stable,
the phonon dispersion curves are calculated.
The results obtained from finite differences using $3\times3$ supercells are shown in figure~\ref{fig:xene_ob14_phonon_dispersion}
for the monolayer and bilayer.
No imaginary frequencies are present;
therefore,
these two pristine structures are predicted to be dynamically stable.
For the hydrogen-terminated systems,
we find that the hydrogen-terminated bilayer is dynamically stable,
whereas the hydrogen-terminated monolayer is not.
This is reflected by the presence of imaginary frequencies for the latter,
but not for the former,
as shown in figures~\ref{S2.8} and~\ref{S2.9}.
Based on these results,
the hydrogen-terminated monolayer is not considered further.
Using \textit{ab initio} molecular dynamics calculations at a temperature of \qty{400}{K} within VASP,
we also verified that the hydrogen-terminated bilayer is thermodynamically stable,
as shown in figure~\ref{S2.10}.

\subsubsection{Electronic properties}
\label{xene_ob14_electronic_properties}

The charge density difference distribution for hydrogen adsorption on the surface of the bilayer is shown in figure~\ref{fig:h_ob14_charge_density_difference}.
There is a significant depletion of electron density around the hydrogen atoms,
and an increase both in the interlayer B-B bonds and in the $p_z$ orbital of the uppermost bipyramid boron atom.
The latter is directed toward the hydrogen atom and is partially hidden within the large depletion region.
The increase in electron density between the interlayer B-B bond is consistent with the slightly smaller distance compared with the bilayer without hydrogen adsorption.

The isosurface level is $1.5\times10^{-4}~e\,a_0^{-3}$.}
\label{fig:h_ob14_charge_density_difference}
\end{figure}

The spin-polarized band structures of the monolayer are shown in the left and middle panels of figure~\ref{fig:monolayer_bilayer_band_structure},
as calculated using the HSE06 functional.
The antiferromagnetic configuration is more favourable than the ferromagnetic configuration by \qty{0.02}{\electronvolt}.
A small band gap of \qty{0.105}{\electronvolt} opens in the antiferromagnetic case.
In the nonmagnetic state,
the system is metallic and exhibits a high density of states at the Fermi level.
This feature is related to flat bands throughout the Brillouin zone,
particularly along the $\mathrm{T}$--$\mathrm{Y}$ direction,
as shown in figures~\ref{S2.12} and~\ref{S2.13}.
Since a large density of states at the Fermi level often suggests that a material is prone to undergo transitions to more stable ground-states,
such as forming superconductors, ferromagnets,
or experiencing structural distortions such as the Jahn--Teller effect,
we tested these aspects, as shown in figure~\ref{S2.14}.
The structure is found to be magnetic,
with a magnetic moment of $0.262~\mu_{\mathrm{B}}$ on each apical boron atom.
For the ferromagnetic case,
fixed-spin calculations show a total magnetic moment of around $2~\mu_{\mathrm{B}}$ for the unit cell,
as shown in figure~\ref{S2.15}.
The density of states of the ferromagnetic structure clearly shows significant spin splitting of the states related to the apical boron atoms,
as shown in the upper panel of figure~\ref{S2.16}.
The density of states for the antiferromagnetic ordering is shown in the lower panel of figure~\ref{S2.16}.

For the antiferromagnetic case,
the spatial distributions of the highest occupied and lowest unoccupied states at a k-point midway along the high-symmetry $\mathrm{T}$--$\mathrm{Y}$ direction are shown in figure~\ref{fig:monolayer_afm_states_spin_density}.
The total spin density is also shown in the same figure.
It is apparent that the apical boron atoms of the bipyramid are responsible for the magnetic behaviour of the system,
where they have a magnetic moment of $0.262~\mu_{\mathrm{B}}$.
The analogous result for the ferromagnetic ordering is shown in figure~\ref{S2.17}.
The energetic preference for antiferromagnetic ordering over ferromagnetic ordering can be understood in terms of the competition between electron hopping and exchange interactions.
In the antiferromagnetic configuration,
virtual hopping between neighbouring magnetic sites is enhanced because electrons can delocalize onto oppositely aligned spins without violating the Pauli exclusion principle,
leading to a greater kinetic-energy lowering.
In contrast, ferromagnetic alignment restricts such virtual hopping processes and reduces the associated kinetic-energy gain.
Therefore, the antiferromagnetic state is energetically more favourable.

On terminating the bilayer structure with hydrogen,

As shown in figure~\ref{fig:h_bilayer_band_dos},

\subsection{Superconducting properties}
\label{2_superconducting}

The conventional superconducting properties of bulk o-\ce{B14},
the monolayer,
and the hydrogen-terminated bilayer are investigated.
The bilayer is not considered due to its semiconducting nature.
We first confirm that the atomic relaxation obtained using Quantum ESPRESSO yields very similar results to those obtained using VASP.
For the hydrogen-terminated bilayer,
the lattice constants $a$ and $b$ are \qty{5.66}{\angstrom} and \qty{6.12}{\angstrom} from Quantum ESPRESSO,
compared with \qty{5.68}{\angstrom} and \qty{6.14}{\angstrom} from VASP.
The interlayer distance is \qty{4.13}{\angstrom} from Quantum ESPRESSO and \qty{4.11}{\angstrom} from VASP,
while the H-B distance is \qty{1.19}{\angstrom} in both cases.

For the bulk phase,
an electron-phonon coupling strength of $\lambda=0.931$ is obtained,
corresponding to an Allen--Dynes superconducting transition temperature of $T_c=\qty{28.2}{K}$.
This value is consistent with the previously reported value of \qty{29.1}{K}~\cite{han2023superconducting}.
The hydrogen-terminated bilayer exhibits a slightly higher electron-phonon coupling strength,
$\lambda=1.01$,
but a lower transition temperature of $T_c=\qty{18.5}{K}$.
This may be attributed to phonon softening effects arising at higher electron-phonon coupling strengths.
The monolayer phase yields $\lambda=0.05$ and a negligible $T_c$,
indicating the absence of significant phonon-mediated superconductivity.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
    \adjustbox{valign=c}{\includegraphics[width=0.49\linewidth]{figures_proj2/fig2.9a.pdf}}%
    \adjustbox{valign=c}{\includegraphics[width=0.49\linewidth]{figures_proj2/fig2.9b.pdf}}%
\caption{Superconducting gap versus temperature for bulk o-\ce{B14} (left)
and hydrogen-terminated bilayer o-\ce{B14} (right).}
\label{fig:ob14_superconducting_gap}
\end{figure}

From the anisotropic Migdal--Eliashberg equations,
the analysis of the superconducting gap evolution further supports these results.
As shown in the left and right panels of figure~\ref{fig:ob14_superconducting_gap},
the superconducting gap closes around $T_c=\qty{31.3}{K}$ for bulk o-\ce{B14}
and around $T_c=\qty{24.3}{K}$ for the hydrogen-terminated bilayer.
Overall, these results suggest that o-\ce{B14} is unlikely to possess conventional superconductivity in the monolayer phase owing to the extremely weak electron-phonon interaction,
whereas the bulk and hydrogen-terminated bilayer possess phonon-mediated superconducting states with transition temperatures in the range of \qtyrange{20}{30}{K}.

\subsubsection*{Renormalization of the dielectric function to account for the vacuum region}

The effective thickness of the 2D systems, $d_{\mathrm{2D}}$,
is required for the dielectric-function renormalization introduced in Section~\ref{2_methods}.
We define it from the difference in the $z$ coordinate of the top and bottom atoms in the system, $dz$,
together with one additional van der Waals radius on each side of the slab.
Thus, $d_{\mathrm{2D}}=dz+2d_{\mathrm{vdW}}$.
For the bare monolayer and bilayer,
the top and bottom atoms are the pyramidal apical boron atoms.
For the hydrogen-terminated bilayer,
the top and bottom atoms are the H atoms.
Here, we use $d_{\mathrm{vdW}}=\qty{1.91}{\angstrom}$ for boron and $d_{\mathrm{vdW}}=\qty{1.2}{\angstrom}$ for hydrogen.
A schematic illustration of the total thickness, $D_{\mathrm{total}}\coloneqq d_{\mathrm{2D}}$,
is shown in figure~\ref{S2.6}.
