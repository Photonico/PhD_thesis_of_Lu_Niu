\section{Toward electromagnetic response in matter}

The previous chapter focused on the ground-state electronic problem.
There, the central question was how electrons arrange themselves under a given external potential.
However, optical properties are not determined by the ground-state alone.
When an electromagnetic field is applied, the electrons are driven away from their static equilibrium, and the system demonstrates a series of responses.

Therefore, before introducing the dielectric function, we need a language that describes both the applied field and the feedback of matter to that field.
That language is electrodynamics.
It tells us which structures are universal, and which parts depend on the specific material.

The present section does not review a complete lecture of electrodynamics.
Instead, we only extract the ingredients required for the later discussion of the dielectric function, the Kramers--Kronig relations, and the optical properties.
We will start from electromagnetic fields in matter, and gradually move toward a frequency-dependent response function that can be connected to the electronic structure.

\subsection{Maxwell equations in matter}

Let us start with the universal part of the problem.
When an electromagnetic field enters a material, it induces charge redistribution inside the medium, and the induced charges further modify the field.
Therefore, the optical response of matter should be described within the framework of macroscopic electrodynamics~\cite{griffiths2023introduction}.
